Description

This project's main objective was to automate aerial grasping with universal jamming grippers, as manually controlling this operation quickly overburdens the drone operator. This project had three distinct goals:

  1. Convert the modeling insights gained here into a gazebo model
  2. Develop and implement a suitable control strategy for the universal jamming gripper
  3. Test the system in simulation

Simulation Model

Starting from the [mental model] developed earlier, an [analogous model] suitable for simulation had to be found. Since gazebo (the robotics simulator) cannot handle soft body dynamics, the gripper's model had to be simplified to represent the important characteristics of the gripper, namely:

  • the transition from soft to rigid
  • the change of volume (shrinking of the membrane) due to the variable air content in the membrane
  • the elastic/springy behavior depending on the air content

A precise model of the gripper's membrane's shape was not seen as a major factor in achieving a faithful simulation model (and it could not be achieved with gazebo alone).

To achieve the requirements, the gripper was modeled as a uniaxial, variable spring acting on a disc shape attached to a prismatic joint. The force to apply to that joint is governed by the law developed previously, which depends on the depth of entry, and the air content in the membrane. A gazebo plugin applies force to the joint and provides feedback on the contact force. It also takes care of necessary ros integration and creates and breaks 'joints' with the payload to grasp and release the payloads. The gripper was then [attached] to a UAV and a simulated depth camera.

Control Strategy

The goal here is to have a reliable control strategy that:

  • guides our UAV effectively towards the payload following a trajectory that preferably avoids obstacles it encounters
  • keep a well-defined contact force between the gripper and the payload while the pumps are running

The trajectory is obtained by repeatedly solving a non-linear optimization problem. This receding-horizon optimization scheme uses a simple kinematic model (the 'dynamics' of the UAV) and a cost function that encourages the desired behaviors. The behaviors herein were:

  • tracking (get closer to the goal)
  • repulsion (stay away from obstacles)
  • perception (keep the goal in the field of view)
  • grasping (approach the goal from the top and minimize motion in the horizontal plane)

Upon contact with the payload, the control scheme is [switched from trajectory control to force control]. During pump activation, a sliding mode controller keeps the contact force close to the desired level. This is an important aspect as any loss in contact force would result in a reduced contact surface and therefore increase the likelihood of a failed grasp.

Virtual Experiment

To conclude, a test scenario was set up where a payload is a priori roughly located in a [parking garage]. There are plenty of obstacles to be avoided by the UAV. The camera is used to refine the goal position as soon as it enters its field of view. In this scenario, the aerial system takes off, travels, refines the location of the payload, [picks it up], transports it to a safe area to be disposed of, and finally lands back on the gripper.
